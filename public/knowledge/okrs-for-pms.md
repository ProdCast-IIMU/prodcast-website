# OKRs for Product Managers

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) were invented at Intel by Andy Grove, brought to Google by John Doerr, and are now the dominant goal-setting system across product-led companies.

For PMs, OKRs are the bridge between company strategy and what the team actually builds.

---

## The Structure

**Objective:** A qualitative, inspiring, time-bound goal. Answers "where are we going?"

**Key Results:** 2–5 quantitative measures that define what success looks like. Answers "how do we know we got there?"

**Rule:** Key Results must be outcomes, not outputs.

---

## Outcomes vs. Outputs

This is the most important distinction in product OKRs.

| Output (Wrong) | Outcome (Right) |
|---|---|
| Ship the new onboarding flow | Increase Day 7 retention from 32% to 45% |
| Launch 3 new features | Increase weekly active usage by 25% |
| Conduct 20 user interviews | Reduce checkout abandonment from 68% to 50% |
| Fix the 5 top bugs | CSAT on checkout flow improves from 3.2 to 4.1 |

*Shipping features is an output. Users getting value is an outcome.*

---

## Writing a Good Objective

A strong Objective is:
- **Qualitative and inspiring** — it motivates the team
- **Time-bound** — quarterly is standard
- **Aligned upward** — it contributes to a company-level objective
- **Not a metric** — the metrics go in the Key Results

**Weak:** "Improve our growth metrics"
**Strong:** "Make ProdCast the go-to resource for PM learning at Indian B-schools"

---

## Writing Good Key Results

A strong Key Result is:
- **Measurable** — you can answer yes or no at the end of the quarter
- **Outcome-based** — it measures user or business impact, not effort
- **Ambitious but achievable** — 70% attainment is considered a success at Google
- **Owned** — someone is responsible for it

**Formula:** Verb + metric + from X to Y

*Examples:*
- "Increase Knowledge Hub monthly active readers from 0 to 500"
- "Grow Instagram followers from 1,200 to 2,500"
- "Achieve NPS of 45+ among event attendees (currently 31)"

---

## The OKR Hierarchy

```
Company OKR
  ↓ aligns to
  Team / Product OKR
    ↓ aligns to
    Individual OKR (optional)
```

Each level's OKRs should directly contribute to the level above. If you can't draw the line from your team's OKR to the company's OKR, the OKR is misaligned.

---

## Grading OKRs

At the end of the quarter, score each Key Result from 0.0 to 1.0:

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1.0 | Fully achieved |
| 0.7 | Strong progress — expected outcome |
| 0.5 | Partial progress — needs review |
| 0.3 | Minimal progress — what went wrong? |
| 0.0 | Not started / failed |

**Google's rule:** If you consistently score 1.0, your OKRs aren't ambitious enough. Target 0.6–0.7.

---

## Common OKR Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Making OKRs a task list**
OKRs are not a sprint backlog. They are outcomes the team commits to achieving through whatever means works.

**Mistake 2: Too many OKRs**
3 Objectives × 3 Key Results = 9 metrics per quarter. That's the maximum. More than that and focus is gone.

**Mistake 3: Annual OKRs with no check-ins**
OKRs require weekly or bi-weekly check-ins. Without check-ins, they become a graveyard of aspirations.

**Mistake 4: Sandbagging**
Setting OKRs you know you'll hit in week 2 of the quarter. OKRs should be uncomfortable.

**Mistake 5: Tying OKR grades to performance reviews**
This incentivises sandbagging. OKRs and HR performance systems should be kept separate.

---

## OKRs vs. KPIs

| OKRs | KPIs |
|---|---|
| Aspirational, change each quarter | Steady-state health metrics, monitored continuously |
| Set by teams collaboratively | Often set top-down |
| Measure progress toward a goal | Measure operational health |
| Acceptable to miss at 30% | Missing a KPI is usually a crisis |

*You need both. OKRs drive change. KPIs keep the lights on.*
