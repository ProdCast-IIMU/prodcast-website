# The Kano Model: What Users Want vs. What Delights Them

The Kano Model, developed by Professor Noriaki Kano in 1984, explains why some features make users ecstatic while others are simply expected — and why you can't treat all features the same way.

---

## The Five Feature Categories

### 1. Basic Needs (Must-Be Quality)
Features users expect so fundamentally that their absence causes extreme dissatisfaction — but their presence goes completely unnoticed.

*Examples:*
- A ride-hailing app shows you where the driver is
- A banking app doesn't lose your money
- An e-commerce site has a working search

**Characteristic:** You can't delight users with these. You can only fail them.

---

### 2. Performance Needs (One-Dimensional)
Features where more = better. The more you deliver, the more satisfied users are.

*Examples:*
- Battery life on a phone
- Delivery speed on an e-commerce platform
- Video quality on a streaming service

**Characteristic:** Linear relationship between performance and satisfaction. These are what users consciously ask for in surveys.

---

### 3. Excitement Needs (Attractive Quality / Delighters)
Features users don't expect and don't ask for — but when present, they produce disproportionate delight. Their absence causes no dissatisfaction.

*Examples:*
- Spotify Wrapped
- iPhone's original pinch-to-zoom
- Zomato's real-time delivery tracking animation

**Characteristic:** Asymmetric upside. A little goes a long way.

---

### 4. Indifferent Features
Features that cause neither satisfaction nor dissatisfaction regardless of whether they're present.

*Examples:*
- The exact shade of blue on a button
- How many server locations a VPN uses (for most users)

**Characteristic:** Stop building these. They're resource sinks.

---

### 5. Reverse Features
Features that actually cause dissatisfaction when present.

*Examples:*
- Autoplay video with sound
- Forced account creation before purchase
- Push notifications you didn't ask for

**Characteristic:** Sometimes the best product decision is to remove a feature.

---

## The Time Decay of Delighters

The most important insight of the Kano Model is that **delighters decay into basics over time.**

- In 2007, multi-touch on iPhone was a delighter
- By 2012, it was a basic need

- In 2018, free same-day delivery was a delighter for Amazon Prime
- By 2023, users expect same-day delivery as a baseline

**Implication:** You must continuously invest in new delighters while maintaining basics. The competitive battleground is always shifting.

---

## Running a Kano Survey

For each feature, ask users two questions:

1. **Functional:** "How would you feel if this feature *was* present?"
2. **Dysfunctional:** "How would you feel if this feature *was not* present?"

Response options: Delighted / Expected it / Neutral / Can live with it / Dislike it

Map the responses to classify each feature.

---

## Applying Kano to Prioritisation

| Category | Prioritisation Logic |
|---|---|
| Basic Needs | Must ship. Non-negotiable. |
| Performance Needs | Invest proportional to competitive gap |
| Delighters | Pick 1–2 per cycle. Protect the surprise. |
| Indifferent | Cut ruthlessly |
| Reverse | Remove immediately |

---

## Kano + RICE

Use the Kano Model to *qualify* features before running RICE scores. A feature that's a Basic Need should be treated differently than a Delighter — even if both score similarly on RICE.

- **Basic Needs:** Ship fast, even if RICE score is moderate. The cost of absence is catastrophic.
- **Delighters:** Protect them even if reach is low. A 5% reach delighter can be worth more in word-of-mouth than a 40% reach basic fix.
