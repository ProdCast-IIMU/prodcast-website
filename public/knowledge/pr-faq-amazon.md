# Working Backwards: Amazon's PR/FAQ

> Start with the press release. If you can't write a crisp one-page announcement of the customer benefit, the product idea isn't ready.

## What is it?

Amazon's PR/FAQ (Press Release / Frequently Asked Questions) is a product development method introduced by Jeff Bezos in the early 2000s. Instead of building first and explaining later, the team writes a fictional press release announcing the product as if it already exists — *before* writing a single line of code.

This forces one discipline: you must articulate customer value clearly upfront. If you can't describe the benefit in a compelling way to a customer, you probably don't understand the product well enough to build it.

## Where is it used?

- Amazon's internal product development process (still used today; new initiatives require a PR/FAQ before getting headcount)
- PM interviews: "Walk me through how you'd define a new product"
- Early-stage startup product strategy (replaces a 40-page requirements document)
- Aligning stakeholders on "what success looks like" before committing to a roadmap
- MBA product strategy presentations

## How it Works

A PR/FAQ has two parts: the Press Release (1 page max) and the FAQ document (typically 3–5 pages). The PR is the vision; the FAQ is where the hard questions live.

---

### The Press Release (1 Page, Max)

The PR must include, in order:

**Headline** — Write it as a real publication would headline it. Must capture the customer benefit in one line.
*Bad: "Amazon launches new logistics feature"*
*Good: "Amazon shoppers can now choose the exact 2-hour delivery window that fits their schedule — no more waiting at home all day"*

**Sub-headline** — One sentence elaborating on who benefits and how.

**The Customer Problem** — What painful problem exists today? Be specific. Name the actual frustration, not the abstract category.

**The Solution** — How does this product solve it? Not "our platform" or "our AI" — the *specific* mechanism.

**A Customer Quote** — Write a fictional but realistic quote from the target customer describing how this changed their life. This forces empathy: would a real person actually say this? If the quote sounds like marketing copy, the product isn't solving a real problem.

**Getting Started** — How would a new customer begin using this? One sentence.

**A Second Quote** — From an internal stakeholder (founder, business unit leader) connecting this to the company's mission.

**Call to Action** — Where does the reader go to learn more or sign up?

---

### The FAQ: Two Sections

**Internal FAQs** — The hardest questions stakeholders will ask before approving investment:

- Why will customers switch from their current solution?
- How is this technically feasible? What are the riskiest assumptions?
- What does it cost to build, and what does success need to look like to justify that cost?
- Who are the real competitors — including non-obvious ones?
- What are the privacy and regulatory implications?
- What happens if this cannibalises an existing product?

**External FAQs** — Questions real customers will ask before using it:

- How much does it cost?
- How is my data handled?
- Does it work with the tools I already use?
- What happens if I want to cancel?
- Is this available in my region?

---

### Why This Works

The PR/FAQ method surfaces two categories of failures early:

**Clarity failures:** If you can't write a clean 1-page press release, your team doesn't agree on what you're building. Better to discover this in a document than after 6 months of development.

**Customer empathy failures:** The customer quote is a forcing function. "Our innovative AI-powered solution transforms how you manage tasks" is not a real human talking. A real human says: "I used to spend Sunday evenings dreading the week ahead. Now I spend 10 minutes Sunday morning and I'm done."

The FAQ section forces honest confrontation with the hardest questions. At Amazon, the review session for a PR/FAQ is a debate — leadership pokes holes until the document either breaks or gets stronger.

## Real-World Example

**Amazon Echo (Alexa) — reconstructed PR/FAQ**

**Headline:** *"Say it aloud: Amazon's new device plays music, answers questions, and controls your smart home — hands-free, from anywhere in the room"*

**The customer problem:** Smart home devices in 2013 required a phone or remote. You couldn't turn on music while cooking with flour-covered hands. You couldn't dim the lights without crossing the room.

**The solution:** A far-field microphone array that hears voice commands from across the room — seven microphones, always listening, without requiring a button press.

**Customer quote:** *"I asked it to add milk to my shopping list while I was standing at the stove. It confirmed and added it. I didn't stop cooking once."*

**Key internal FAQs from the original process:**
- "Why won't people think it's creepy that it's always listening?" → Addressed through transparent LED indicator design and offline processing of wake word only
- "What's the killer use case?" → Music. Everything else is a bonus. Make music setup frictionless.
- "How do you monetise this?" → Device itself + Amazon ecosystem lock-in (Prime, Amazon Music, Alexa Skills marketplace)

Echo went on to sell 22 million units in its first three years — a product that started as a PR/FAQ debate in a conference room.

## Practice Case

**Your assignment:** You're a PM at a major Indian bank (SBI, HDFC, Kotak). You want to launch a savings product specifically for Tier 3 city users who are transitioning from cash-first to digital payments. They have smartphones but have never used a savings account or digital banking product.

Write a 1-page PR/FAQ:

1. **Headline** — what is the single customer benefit in one line?
2. **Customer problem** — be specific about what this user's life looks like today without the product
3. **Solution** — what is the specific mechanism? (Don't say "easy digital banking")
4. **Customer quote** — write one that a real person in this segment would actually say
5. **One internal FAQ** — pick the hardest question a skeptical executive would ask, and answer it honestly
