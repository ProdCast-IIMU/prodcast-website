# The North Star Metric

A North Star Metric (NSM) is the single metric that best captures the core value your product delivers to customers. It aligns every team — engineering, design, marketing, growth — around one shared measure of progress.

---

## Why One Metric?

Most products track dozens of KPIs. The problem: when everything is important, nothing is.

The NSM forces a team to answer the hardest question in product: **what does it actually mean for a user to get value from us?**

---

## The Three Tests of a Good NSM

A valid North Star Metric must pass all three:

1. **It measures value delivered to the user** — not revenue, not growth
2. **It predicts long-term business health** — when it goes up, revenue follows
3. **It's actionable by the team** — teams can run experiments to move it

---

## Famous Examples

| Company | North Star Metric |
|---|---|
| Spotify | Time spent listening per user |
| Airbnb | Nights booked |
| Facebook | Daily Active Users (DAUs) |
| Slack | Messages sent per active team |
| Netflix | Hours of content watched per subscriber |
| LinkedIn | Weekly active users in professional context |
| Duolingo | Daily Active Users completing a lesson |
| Zepto | Orders delivered in under 10 minutes |

---

## How to Find Your NSM

**Step 1: Define your core value exchange**
What is the moment when a user truly gets value from your product?

For Spotify, it's when music is playing. For Zepto, it's when groceries arrive in 10 minutes. For LinkedIn, it's when a professional connection leads to an opportunity.

**Step 2: Express it as a rate or frequency**
The NSM should reflect *sustained* value, not a one-time event.

- ❌ "Total orders placed" (cumulative, always goes up)
- ✅ "Orders per active user per week" (reflects ongoing value)

**Step 3: Test it against the three criteria**
Does it measure user value? Does it predict business health? Can teams move it?

## The NSM Tree

The NSM sits at the top of a metric tree. Below it are **input metrics** — the levers teams can pull to move the NSM.

```
North Star Metric
├── Input Metric 1 (e.g. Acquisition)
│   ├── Sub-metric A
│   └── Sub-metric B
├── Input Metric 2 (e.g. Activation)
│   ├── Sub-metric A
│   └── Sub-metric B
└── Input Metric 3 (e.g. Retention)
    ├── Sub-metric A
    └── Sub-metric B
```

**Example for a food delivery app:**

```
Weekly orders per retained user (NSM)
├── New user acquisition rate
├── Onboarding completion rate
├── Reorder rate (Day 7, Day 30)
└── Average delivery time (quality driver)
```

## Common NSM Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Using revenue as the NSM**
Revenue is a *result* of value, not value itself. Teams optimising for revenue can extract short-term value while destroying long-term trust.

**Mistake 2: Choosing an easily gameable metric**
If the metric can be inflated without actually delivering value, it's the wrong metric. *Example: Total sessions (users opening and closing the app counts as a session).*

**Mistake 3: Picking a metric no team owns**
If it's nobody's job to move the metric, it won't move.

**Mistake 4: Never revisiting it**
As products evolve, the core value exchange can shift. Reassess your NSM annually.

---

## NSM vs. OKRs

The NSM is **strategic** — it rarely changes. OKRs are **tactical** — they set quarterly targets on the input metrics that drive the NSM. Together, they create alignment from the C-suite to the individual contributor.
