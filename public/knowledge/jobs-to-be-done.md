# Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)

Jobs To Be Done is a framework for understanding *why* customers buy or use a product — not who they are demographically, but what problem they are trying to solve in their life.

The core insight: **people don't buy products. They hire them to do a job.**

---

## The Milkshake Story

Clayton Christensen's classic example: a fast food chain wanted to sell more milkshakes. They studied demographics — age, income, flavour preference. Sales didn't improve.

Then they studied *when* and *why* people bought milkshakes. They found that 40% of milkshakes were bought before 9am by solo commuters who had a long, boring drive ahead.

The job: **"Keep me occupied and not hungry during my morning commute."**

Competitors weren't other milkshakes — they were bananas, bagels, and coffee. The fix: make the shake thicker and harder to finish fast. Sales improved.

*Demographics tell you who bought. JTBD tells you why.*

## The Three Types of Jobs

### Functional Jobs
The core practical task the user is trying to accomplish.

*"I need to transfer money to my sister in another city."*

### Emotional Jobs
How the user wants to feel while doing the job.

*"I want to feel confident that the transfer was safe and arrived."*

### Social Jobs
How the user wants to be perceived by others.

*"I want to look financially responsible to my family."*

The best products address all three. Most products only address the functional job.

---

## The Job Statement Formula

A well-formed job statement has three parts:

**[Verb] + [Object] + [Context/Constraint]**

*Examples:*
- "Help me **track** my **spending** **without having to log every transaction manually**"
- "Help me **find** a **plumber** **who is trustworthy and available today**"
- "Help me **prepare** for a **PM interview** **in under 2 weeks**"

The context and constraint are what differentiate the job from a generic need.

## The Forces of Progress

When a user "hires" a new product, four forces are at play:

```
Pushing toward change:          Pulling toward new product:
  Frustration with current   →    Attraction of the better solution
  solution

Pulling toward status quo:      Pushing against change:
  Habit / familiarity        ←    Anxiety about switching
```

To win, your product must make the **push + pull** forces stronger than the **anxiety + habit** forces.

This is why features alone don't drive adoption. You need to reduce switching anxiety and leverage habit formation.

## JTBD vs. Personas

| Personas | JTBD |
|---|---|
| Based on demographics | Based on the situation and goal |
| Static snapshot | Dynamic — same person has different jobs at different times |
| "25–35 year old urban professional" | "Help me make a confident decision fast" |
| Often leads to over-building | Focuses on the minimum needed to get the job done |

Use both: personas help with empathy, JTBD helps with scope.

## How to Run a JTBD Interview

The goal is to uncover the *real* job, not the surface request.

**Key questions:**
1. "Walk me through the last time you used [product/solution]. What were you doing right before?"
2. "What was frustrating about how you were solving this before?"
3. "When you first decided to try [product], what were you hoping it would do for you?"
4. "Was there a moment when you almost didn't switch? What held you back?"
5. "How do you know when you've done this job successfully?"

**Listen for:** the moment of frustration (the push), the aspiration (the pull), and the anxiety that almost stopped them.

---

## Applying JTBD to Product Decisions

**Feature scoping:** "What job does this feature do? Does it do the job better than what users are doing today?"

**Positioning:** "We're not competing with X — we're competing with whatever users are using to get this job done, including doing nothing."

**Onboarding:** "Help users complete their first job successfully as fast as possible — then they'll come back."

**Pricing:** "Price to the value of the job done, not the features delivered."
