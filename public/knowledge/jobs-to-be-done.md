# Jobs To Be Done

> People don't buy products. They hire them to get a job done. Understanding the job reveals your real competition — and it's rarely who you think.

## What is it?

Jobs To Be Done (JTBD) is a theory of customer motivation developed by Harvard Business School professor Clayton Christensen. The central insight: customers aren't buying your product because of its features. They're hiring it because they have a specific job to do in their life, and your product is the best available candidate.

The JTBD lens reframes every product decision — from what to build, to who competes with you, to how to price.

## Where is it used?

- User research to uncover the *real* motivation behind product usage
- Positioning and messaging (defining what job the product does in one sentence)
- Identifying true competitors (not always obvious from the category)
- Feature scoping: "Does this feature help users get the job done better, or does it just add surface area?"
- PM interviews: "How do you understand your users' needs?"

## How it Works

### The Milkshake Story

This is the most famous JTBD illustration. A fast food chain wanted to sell more milkshakes. They studied demographics — age, income, flavour preference. Nothing moved.

Then they studied *when* and *why* people bought milkshakes. They found that 40% of milkshakes were sold before 9am to solo commuters with a long, boring drive ahead.

The job: **"Keep me occupied and not hungry during my 45-minute commute."**

Competitors weren't other milkshakes. They were bananas (done in 2 minutes, too fast), bagels (messy, need two hands), and nothing (hungry and bored by 9:30am). The fix: make the shake thicker and harder to finish fast. Sales improved.

Demographics told them *who* bought. JTBD told them *why*.

### The Three Layers of a Job

Every job has three layers, and the best products address all three:

**Functional Job** — the core practical task the user is trying to accomplish.
*"I need to send money to my sister in another city."*

**Emotional Job** — how the user wants to feel while doing the job.
*"I want to feel confident the transfer is safe and will arrive instantly."*

**Social Job** — how the user wants to be perceived by others.
*"I want to look like someone who handles money responsibly."*

Most products only address the functional job. The ones that win on all three tend to dominate their categories. WhatsApp's near-zero failure rate addresses the emotional job (certainty of delivery). GPay's instant confirmation screen plus UPI ID addresses the social job (you look tech-savvy to your family).

### Writing a Job Statement

A well-formed job statement has three parts: **[Verb] + [Object] + [Context/Constraint]**

The context and constraint are what differentiate the job from a generic need:

- "Help me **track** my **spending** **without logging every transaction manually**"
- "Help me **find** a **trusted plumber** **available today in my area**"
- "Help me **prepare** for a **PM interview** **while working a full-time job**"

Generic need: "track spending." Job: "track spending without manual effort." These lead to entirely different product solutions.

### The Forces of Progress

When a user "hires" a new product, four forces are at play simultaneously:

**Pushing toward change:**
- Frustration with the current solution
- Attraction of the better alternative

**Pulling toward the status quo:**
- Anxiety about switching (What if the new thing is worse?)
- Habit and inertia (The old thing is good enough)

To win adoption, push + pull must outweigh anxiety + inertia. This is why features alone don't drive switching. Reducing switching anxiety — through free trials, easy imports, fast onboarding — matters as much as the product itself.

---

### Running a JTBD Interview

The goal is to surface the *real* job, not what users say they want. Standard questions miss this because they're too hypothetical. JTBD interviews are retrospective:

1. "Walk me through the last time you used [product]. What were you doing right before you opened it?"
2. "What were you trying to get done when you first looked for something like this?"
3. "What was frustrating about how you were solving it before?"
4. "Was there a moment when you almost didn't switch to [product]? What held you back?"
5. "How do you know when you've done this job successfully?"

Listen for: the moment of frustration (the push), the aspiration (the pull), and the anxiety that nearly stopped them.

## Real-World Example

**Nykaa: Hired for a job beyond beauty products**

Nykaa's early growth was puzzling — users would spend 45 minutes on the app browsing, but convert at only 8%. Traditional e-commerce analysis said "fix the funnel." JTBD analysis revealed something different.

Users weren't primarily hiring Nykaa *to buy makeup*. They were hiring it *to learn how to apply it correctly for their skin tone and occasion*. The "job" had a research/education component that came before purchase.

Once Nykaa understood this job, they invested in beauty tutorials, product-specific how-to videos, and editorial content. These features drove purchase intent by helping users complete the full job (learn + buy). Average session time went up. Conversion followed.

Their real competition wasn't just Myntra — it was YouTube beauty tutorials.

## Practice Case

**Scenario:** You're a PM at a startup building a food planning app (think: weekly meal planning + grocery list generation). Surveys show users love the concept but barely use it after the first week.

Apply JTBD:
1. What job are users *actually* hiring this app to do? List all three layers (functional, emotional, social).
2. What were they "firing" when they started using your app? What solution were they replacing?
3. What forces of inertia are pulling them back to the old solution after week one?
4. Based on this, what single feature change would most help users complete the job?
